Scalar and Vector Quantities

  • Scalar
    • A Scalar is quantity that has Magnitude, but not a direction associated with it.
    • Magnitude – A numerical value with units.
    • Example:
        • Scalar Quantities Magnitude
        Speed 20 m/s
        Mass 1 kg
        Volume 10 m^3
        Distance 1m
    • Scalar Addition follows ordinary arithmetic procedures.
    • Example:
      • Mass: 10kg + 5kg = 15kg
      • Length: 20m - 10m = 10m

  • Vector
    • A Vector is quantity that has both Magnitude and Direction.
    • Example:
        • Scalar Quantities Magnitude Direction
        Speed 20 m/s North
        Mass 10 m/s East
        Volume 5 N West
        Distance 10 m South
    • Vector Addition uses special methods which are not arithmetic. The particular method used depends on the orientation of the vectors and how many vectors are being considered.

  • Basics on Vectors
      A. Vector Notation
    • Vectors are typically represented by a Capital Bold Letter or drawing an Arrow (→) above the symbol. The arrow is used to convey direction and magnitude.
      A. Graphical Representation
    • Vector quantity is represented graphically by an arrow.
      • The length represents magnitude.
      • The arrow faces the direction of motion.

      • Specifying Directions of Vectors:
        • Method 1: Use the angle that the vector makes with the "zero degree reference line".
        • Method 2: Use the geographical directions.

      1. Graphical Solution
    • a. Parallelogram – for two vectors that are non-collinear but coplanar.
    • b. Polygon (Tip-to-Tail) Method - suggested to be used for two or more vectors which are non-collinear but coplanar.
      • The goal is to draw a mini version of the vectors to give an accurate picture of the magnitude and direction:
        • 1. Pick appropriate scale
        • 2. Use a ruler & protractor, draw 1st vector to scale in appropriate direction
        • 3. Start at tip of 1st vector, draw 2nd vector to scale.
        • 4. Connect the vectors starting at the tail end of the 1st and ending with the tip of the last vector. This is the sum of the vectors; it’s called the resultant vector, R
        • 5. Measure the magnitude of R with a ruler and convert this length to its actual amount and unit.
        • 6. Measure the direction of R with a protractor and add this value along with the direction after the magnitude.

    • a. Algebraic Method – for vectors which are collinear.

        • C = 60N due West
        • D = 50N due East
